Whay is Research done?

Research is a process of seeking out answers to a specific problem. It can be conducted for several purposes, such as to understand a phenomenon, behavior, or test a theory. Research is conducted systematically, and it adds to a body of knowledge and supports many theories.

  1. Reseach is done for advancement - which is already known should be evaluated continuously

    By continuously evaluating what is already known, research not only safeguards the credibility of existing knowledge but also ensures its relevance and utility in a constantly evolving world. This cyclical process of discovery and reassessment forms the backbone of scientific progress.

    1. Research as a Tool for Advancement:
      • Expanding Knowledge: Research seeks to push the boundaries of what we know by uncovering
      • Driving Innovation: Advancements in technology, medicine, social science, and other fields often depend on rigorous research to propose and test new ideas.
    2. Continuous Evaluation of Existing Knowledge:
      • Validity Over Time: Knowledge may become outdated or inaccurate due to new discoveries, changes in context, or advancements in methodologies. Regular re-evaluation ensures the reliability of existing concepts.
      • Testing Assumptions: Continuous evaluation challenges old assumptions, which might no longer hold true as the field evolves.
      • Replication Studies: Reproducing past studies helps confirm findings and ensures their applicability to current contexts or diverse populations.
    3. Preventing Stagnation:
      • Without ongoing evaluation, science and knowledge risk stagnation. Reevaluating what is known keeps the field dynamic and responsive to new challenges.
    4. Applications of Continuous Evaluation:
      • Improved Practices: Revisiting known principles can improve techniques, tools, or policies, leading to better outcomes (e.g., revising medical guidelines based on new data).
      • Identifying Gaps: Analyzing what is known can reveal areas that require further exploration or have been overlooked.
      • Ethical and Social Relevance: Reassessment ensures that previously accepted practices align with current ethical standards and societal needs.
    5. Example Scenarios:
      • Medicine:Treatments or drugs that were once considered best practice are regularly evaluated through clinical trials to ensure they remain effective or to develop superior alternatives.
      • Technology: Existing algorithms or systems are reviewed to adapt to newer challenges, such as cybersecurity threats or environmental sustainability.
      • Social Sciences: Sociological theories are re-examined in the light of new societal changes or cultural shifts

  2. Reseach is done for future knowledge in particular area

    By focusing on future knowledge, research not only seeks to solve today’s problems but also anticipates tomorrow’s challenges, ensuring continuous progress and preparedness in a rapidly evolving world.

    1. Generating New Knowledge:
      • Uncovering the Unknown: Research aims to explore unanswered questions, address gaps in knowledge, and investigate new areas that have not yet been studied.
      • Building on Existing Foundations: While research often starts with what is already known, its primary goal is to go beyond that, developing theories, methods, or solutions that push boundaries.
    2. Preparing for the Future:
      • Anticipating Needs: Research often seeks to address future challenges, whether in science, technology, health, or social systems.
        • Example: Climate change research anticipates future environmental shifts and prepares strategies for mitigation and adaptation.
      • Innovation and Development: By exploring new possibilities, research lays the groundwork for future technologies, treatments, or societal advancements.
    3. Specialization in a Particular Area:
      • Focused Exploration: Research is typically concentrated on specific areas to allow for in-depth analysis and expertise development.
        • Example: In medicine, research into gene therapy focuses on future treatment options for genetic disorders.
      • Cross-Disciplinary Impact: Insights gained in one area can often influence other fields, fostering innovation through interdisciplinary collaboration.
    4. Long-Term Impact:
      • Knowledge Repository: Research contributes to a growing repository of information that future generations of scientists, educators, and policymakers can draw upon.
      • Informed Decision-Making: Policymakers and leaders use the knowledge generated by research to make informed decisions that shape the future.
    5. Examples of Research for Future Knowledge:
      • Astronomy: Exploring distant planets or galaxies to understand the potential for future exploration or the origins of the universe.
      • Artificial Intelligence: Research into machine learning algorithms for future applications in automation, healthcare, or education.
      • Public Health: Studies on emerging diseases to prepare vaccines or strategies for future outbreaks.
    6. Ethical and Societal Considerations:
      • Responsibility to Future Generations: Research must consider the long-term implications of its findings, ensuring they are used ethically and sustainably.
      • Balancing Curiosity with Utility: While some research explores purely theoretical questions, much of it is also designed to have practical applications in the future.
  3. Reseach is done to increase understanding

    By increasing understanding, research fulfills a critical role in both intellectual and practical development, empowering individuals and societies to navigate and improve the world around them.

    1. Enhancing Knowledge:
      • Filling Knowledge Gaps: Research identifies and addresses areas where understanding is incomplete or unclear.
      • Clarifying Complex Concepts: By investigating intricate topics, research provides clarity and makes complex ideas more accessible.
    2. Building a Foundation for Action:
      • Informed Decision-Making: Greater understanding enables individuals, organizations, and governments to make evidence-based decisions.
        • Example: Research on economic trends helps policymakers design effective fiscal policies.
      • Practical Applications: Improved understanding leads to better tools, technologies, and strategies in various domains, from medicine to engineering.
    3. Exploring the "Why" and "How":
      • Causal Relationships: Research often seeks to explain why certain events occur and how various factors interact.
        • Example: Understanding how climate change affects ecosystems informs conservation efforts.
      • Predictive Insights: By increasing understanding, research helps predict future outcomes and trends.
    4. Advancing Theoretical Frameworks:
      • Refining Existing Theories: Research tests and enhances current theories, ensuring they remain relevant and robust.
      • Developing New Models: As understanding grows, new frameworks and models emerge to explain phenomena.
    5. Encouraging Curiosity and Exploration:
      • Intrinsic Value of Knowledge: Beyond practical applications, research satisfies the human desire to know and understand the world.
        • Example: Astronomical research may not have immediate practical benefits but fulfills the quest to understand the universe.
      • Stimulating Innovation: Increased understanding often sparks new ideas, leading to innovations that were not initially anticipated.
    6. Examples Across Fields:
      • Medicine: Research into disease mechanisms increases understanding of how illnesses develop and how to treat them effectively.
      • Social Sciences: Studies on cultural behaviors increase understanding of societal dynamics and human interactions.
      • Technology: Research on materials science deepens understanding of physical properties, enabling advancements in construction, electronics, and manufacturing.

Research Ethics

First, What is Ethics?.. Ethics are a system of moral principles that guide people's decisions and actions. They help people determine what is right and wrong, and how to treat others with respect and fairness. Ethics are also known as moral philosophy.

And Research ethics are a set of principles and guidelines that ensure research is conducted in an ethical and responsible manner. Some key ethical considerations in research include:

  • Research ethics is the principles and guidelines that govern the conduct of research, ensuring respect for the dignity, rights, and welfare of the participants and society at large.
  • This field of ethics is important because it identifies the potential harm that could be caused by research and emphasizes integrity and accountability in research processes and responsible diffusion of knowledge.
  • Research ethics dictate that researchers should adhere to honesty, respect, and responsibility. This way, research studies will contribute positively to the advancement of knowledge while ensuring the rights and welfare of all individuals involved.
  • Ethical research not only strengthens the credibility and reliability of scientific inquiry but also encourages public trust in the research process, thus serving the greater good of society.

Objectives in Research Ethics

To protect Human participants.

This focuses on ensuring the safety, dignity, rights, and well-being of individuals who take part in studies. This involves implementing ethical practices and safeguards throughout the research process.

To ensure that research is conducted in a way that serves interests of individuals, group and/or society as a whole.

In research emphasizes the broader purpose of generating benefits that extend beyond the research process. Here’s how it relates to different stakeholders:

  1. Individuals
    • Direct Benefits: Research can provide direct benefits to participants, such as improved treatments in clinical trials, access to new interventions, or personal insights (e.g., genetic research).
    • Respect for Dignity: By focusing on ethical principles, research upholds individuals' dignity, autonomy, and rights.
  2. Groups
    • Addressing Group Needs: Studies often focus on specific populations (e.g., cultural groups, communities, or demographic categories) to identify their unique challenges and propose solutions, such as improved healthcare access or social policies.
    • Reducing Inequality: Research can advocate for marginalized or underserved groups by highlighting disparities and proposing evidence-based recommendations.
  3. Society as a Whole
    • Knowledge Advancement: Research contributes to the overall body of knowledge, fostering scientific, technological, and social progress that benefits humanity.
    • Policy and Practice: Findings can inform policies, educational programs, or societal norms, helping address global challenges like climate change, public health, or social justice.
    • Ethical and Sustainable Development: By serving society, research aligns with long-term goals such as sustainable development, equity, and ethical innovation.

  1. Examining Specific Research Activities
    • Assessment of Ethical Standards: This involves scrutinizing research designs, methodologies, and protocols to confirm they align with ethical guidelines.
    • Case-by-Case Analysis: Each study is unique and must be reviewed individually to identify potential ethical concerns specific to its context, objectives, and participants.
  2. Management of Risk
    • Identifying Potential Risks: Risks may include physical harm, psychological stress, social stigmatization, or economic disadvantages for participants.
    • Mitigating Risks: Researchers must develop strategies to minimize risks, such as safe procedures, counseling support, or contingency plans.
    • Balancing Risk and Benefit: The potential benefits of the research must outweigh the risks to participants or society.
  3. Protection of Confidentiality
    • Data Security: Safeguards must be implemented to protect participants’ private information from breaches, such as encryption or anonymization.
    • Limited Access: Only authorized individuals should access sensitive data to prevent misuse.
    • Respecting Privacy: Participants should feel confident that their personal information will not be shared or published without explicit permission.
  4. Process of Informed Consent
    • Transparency: Participants must receive clear and comprehensive information about the study’s purpose, procedures, risks, and benefits.
    • Voluntary Participation: Consent must be given freely without coercion, ensuring participants are not pressured or manipulated into agreeing.
    • Ongoing Consent: Informed consent isn’t a one-time event; participants should be allowed to ask questions or withdraw consent at any point during the study.

Priciples of Research Ethics

  1. Honesty
    • Honesty in reporting results, whether these results support the hypothesis or desired outcomes, is necessary for scientific integrity
    • Example of honestly
      • A group of researchers is studying long-term smoking effects on lung health to determine whether smokers are at an increased risk of developing COPD compared to non-smokers. The purpose of the study is to generate helpful information for public health in terms of smoking-related illnesses. The research team tracks smokers and non-smokers through several years, monitoring their lung function and health. As the study progresses, they found that most of the people in the smoking group fail to develop COPD in the duration of the study. Rather than manipulating and selectively reporting data to try and make smoking appear more injurious, the researchers have included all data points and indicated that although the risk is increased, not everyone who smokes will go on to develop lung disease.
  2. Integrity
    • Researchers are responsible for reporting data and findings truthfully. This means avoiding practices such as data fabrication and falsification.
    • Example
      • In the long-term effects of smoking on lung health study, it was quickly realized that a large proportion of participants had incorrectly reported their smoking history. Rather than exclude this data to artificially inflate the results, they included it while stating the potential implications of the inaccuracies. By openly mentioning this in their publication, they demonstrated integrity by taking truthfulness over favorable results and ensured that their findings represent the complexities of smoking behavior and its health consequences.
  3. Objectivity
    • Objectivity in research ethics refers to the adherence by researchers to carry out their work without bias, prejudice, or personal interest so that findings, interpretations, and conclusions are unbiased and not influenced by other external factors. This principle is central to the integrity, credibility, and trustworthiness of the research process and outcomes.
    • Example
      • The study that tried to analyze the long-run effects of smoking on the lungs was done by making an unbiased analysis using previously defined criteria for the analysis of data. They statistically analyzed the health outcomes without bringing in their personal belief systems regarding smoking. They found that even the non-smokers suffered from respiratory problems and published those findings as well along with the increased risk of COPD in smokers. The authors demonstrated an objective approach by representing the data with no personal biases to alter the interpretation.
  4. Inform consent
    • Respecting individuals involves obtaining informed consent from research participants. Before participating in a study, individuals should be provided with clear, comprehensive information about the research purpose, procedures, risks, benefits, and their right to withdraw at any time. This empowers participants to make knowledgeable decisions about their involvement.
    • Example
      • Before participating in any study those potential participants were provided with a detailed information sheet outlining the study's purpose, procedures, potential risks and benefits, and their right to withdraw at any time without penalty. The researchers explained the informed consent process verbally and encouraged questions. After ensuring that participants understood the information, they signed a consent form, indicating their voluntary agreement to participate in the study. This process ensured that participants were fully aware of what their involvement entailed before proceeding.
  5. Respect for person/respondent
    • Respect for respondents is a core ethical principle of research that requires that respect be accorded to every participant at every stage of the research process. This will ensure ethical conduct, ensure trust, and provide reliable and valid data.
    • Example
      • In the study of smoking effects on lung health, the researchers ensured that all participants gave informed consent. They explained the purpose of the study, potential risks, and benefits clearly to the participants. A confidentiality policy was implemented in order to protect the identity and personal information of participants. After the study, the researchers provided participants with access to smoking cessation resources and health support, indicating a commitment to the well-being of respondents and the contribution of respondents to the research.
  6. Benificence

    The principle of beneficence means that researchers should aim to do good and promote the well-being of research participants. This involves maximizing potential benefits while minimizing potential harms.

    • Example: A researcher studying a new treatment for a disease must ensure that the treatment is safe and effective for participants. They must also weigh the potential benefits of the treatment against the risks involved, ensuring that the benefits outweigh the risks.

  7. Non-maleficence /Protecting the subjects (Human)

  8. Responsible publication

    Responsible publication means that researchers must present their research findings accurately and honestly. This includes avoiding plagiarism, fabricating data, and selectively reporting results.

    • Example: A researcher studying the effectiveness of a new drug should report all of their findings, including any negative results. They should also ensure that their research methods are clearly described so that other researchers can replicate their study.

  9. Protecting anonymity

    Protecting anonymity means that researchers should not collect or use any information that could identify research participants. This helps to protect participants' privacy and prevent potential harm or discrimination.

    • Example: A researcher conducting a survey about sensitive topics like sexual behavior or drug use should not ask for participants' names or other identifying information. They should also ensure that any data they collect is anonymized before it is analyzed or shared.

  10. Confidentiality

    Confidentiality means that researchers should keep all information about research participants private. This includes protecting participants' responses, personal details, and any other sensitive information that is collected during the study.

    • Example: A researcher conducting interviews with people about their experiences with mental illness should keep all of the information they collect confidential. They should not share this information with anyone else without the participant's consent.

Five general categories of unethical behavior in research can be listed.

Unethical behavior in research undermines the integrity and credibility of the scientific process. It not only tarnishes the reputation of individuals and institutions but also erodes public trust in scientific findings. To address this critical issue, it is essential to categorize and understand the various forms such behavior can take. This discussion will outline five general categories of unethical behavior in research, providing a framework for recognizing and addressing these violations to uphold ethical standards in the scientific community.

  1. Falsification of Data

    Falsification of data occurs when researchers manipulate or alter research findings to present a desired outcome. This can involve changing existing data, omitting data that doesn't fit the desired narrative, or fabricating data altogether.

    The common understanding is transforming in some way - this implies changing, omitting, or fabricating data to fit a desired conclusion. This definition clearly establishes the absence of truth as a deliberate act – hence sanctioning all kinds of research misconduct such as falsification as malicious.

    • Example: Can you imagine this, you're baking a cake, but you want it to look perfect, so you add extra frosting to cover up some cracks. That's like falsifying data - you're changing the results to make them look better, even if it's not true.
    • Example: A researcher changes the results of their own work in order to comply with the survey’s hypothesis instead of proceeding to analyze the data collected.
    • Consequences: Falsification of data is a serious breach of research ethics. It can lead to the publication of false findings, mislead other researchers, and potentially harm individuals or society.
  2. Failure to Credit Others: Stealing Ideas and Insights

    Failure to credit others involves using someone else's ideas, research, or words without proper attribution. This includes failing to cite sources correctly, presenting someone else's work as one's own, or neglecting to acknowledge collaborators who contributed significantly to the research.

    As part of good research practice, should acknowledge where the work of other authors, work, or sources has been copied, adapted, or incorporated or contributed within a research thesis. Although this act may not be extensive with regards to protocol violation it offends possession and violators ethical principles.

    • Example: You're writing a story, and you borrow a cool idea from a friend, but you forget to mention their name. That's like not giving credit to others in research.
    • Example: Suppressing the work or contributions by other researchers’ methods, ideas, or findings includes references to their work.
    • Consequences: Failure to credit others is considered plagiarism, which is a serious academic offense. It undermines the originality and integrity of research, and it can damage the reputation of both the researcher and the institution.
  3. Plagiarism: Taking Credit for Someone Else's Work

    Plagiarism is a specific form of failure to credit others. It involves presenting someone else's work, ideas, or words as one's own, without proper attribution. Plagiarism can take various forms, including:

    - Direct Copying: Copying text word-for-word from a source without using quotation marks or providing a citation.

    - Paraphrasing Without Attribution: Rewording someone else's ideas without giving credit to the original source.

    - Submitting Someone Else's Work: Submitting a paper or project that was written by someone else, claiming it as one's own.

    Copying someone else's work, ideas, or expression and not crediting the owner is in simple terms copying. In the event that a person feels betrayed and in need of vengeance there could be severe repercussions including disoriented loyalty and irretrievable losses for either bodily functions or societal approval.

    • Example: This is like copying your friend's homework and turning it in as your own. It's taking someone else's work and pretending it's yours.
    • Example: Retaining notes from a research paper or website that was not properly cited nor located by the author and possessing no knowledge of consulting the writer creates confusion especially because websites are expected to have annotations.
    • Consequences: Plagiarism is a serious academic offense. It can result in failing grades, expulsion from school, or even legal action.
  4. Conflicts of Interest: Prioritizing Personal Gain Over Research Integrity

    Conflicts of interest arise when a researcher's personal interests, financial gains, or relationships could potentially bias their research findings or influence their decisions.

    Individuals in authority, governance, or management roles possess awareness of what a conflict of interest means since no company or party may object to direct competition against another. Self-financed research is still subject to study leave assumptions; however, in this circumstance, there are significant ethical boundaries

    • Example: Purchasing controls funding or stock in turn using the resources belonging to the company or party itself to conduct experiments.
    • Consequences: Plagiarism is a serious academic offense. It can result in failing grades, expulsion from school, or even legal action.

    Biased design and interpretation due to outside influence occurs when external factors, such as funding sources, political pressures, or personal biases, influence the design, execution, or interpretation of research.
